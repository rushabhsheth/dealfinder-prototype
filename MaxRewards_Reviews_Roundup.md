# MaxRewards — Review Roundup & Summary

_Compiled June 16, 2026. MaxRewards is a credit-card-management app that links your card accounts, auto-activates merchant offers (Amex/Chase/Citi/BofA, etc.) and quarterly bonus categories, tracks statement credits, and recommends the best card to use. The paid tier ("Gold") is "set your own price," roughly $54–$144/yr ($5–$25/mo)._

> **Note on Reddit:** Reddit pages can't be fetched directly through this tool, so verbatim Reddit posts aren't quoted below. Where Reddit sentiment is referenced, it comes through secondary sources (blog comment threads and App Store reviewers who cite Reddit). The richest first-hand user reviews here come from the Apple App Store, plus three independent blog reviews and their comment sections.

## Recurring themes

### What people like
- **One dashboard for many cards.** The single most praised feature — balances, credit utilization, rewards, transactions, and credit scores across multiple issuers in one place. Repeatedly called the main reason to consider paying.
- **"Best card to use" recommendations.** Power users with 7–13 cards say they open it multiple times a day to decide which card to use at a given merchant.
- **Automatic offer activation.** When it works, auto-adding Amex/Chase/etc. offers and quarterly bonus categories genuinely saves time and money (one tester captured ~$127 in 3 months; MaxRewards cites avg. $662/yr for Gold members).
- **Custom point valuations.** You can set your own per-point values so recommendations reflect what points are actually worth to you — more flexible than some competitors.
- **Statement-credit / welcome-bonus tracking.** Helps recover credits people would otherwise forget.

### What people complain about
- **Syncing breaks constantly** — especially Chase, also Amex, Citi, Discover, Capital One. Cards disconnect and need repeated re-linking/2FA. The most common complaint by far.
- **Inaccurate data.** Wrong earn rates (e.g., showing 1x dining when the card earns 3x), misidentified cards (a secured card shown as a premium rewards card), missed credits, incorrect welcome-bonus spend, omitted bonuses. Undermines the app's core purpose.
- **Price.** Many feel Gold is overpriced for what is "basically a data-consolidation app," with a $54–$84/yr floor and up to $300/yr. A 2025 blogger called it "too glitchy and too expensive to recommend."
- **Security / login model.** It uses your bank login credentials rather than a fully tokenized connection. Triggers fraud locks at Chase/Discover/Citi for some users, and a developer in the space alleged raw credentials are stored server-side. MaxRewards counters that credentials are encrypted and not stored. Several reviewers refuse to link accounts over this.
- **Aggressive/early paywall & onboarding friction.** Asks for bank info and pushes a subscription before you can see core functionality; hard-to-find account deletion; slow/no support responses for some.
- **The Amex "one card" offer conflict.** Activating an Amex offer on one card can block using it on another Amex card you own.
- **No merchant brand search** (location-only) and **app-only notifications** (no email alerts).
- **U.S.-only** — frustrates Canadian users.

### Blog review — Lazy Points (Oct 2025), "Worth $9 a Month?"
**TL;DR:** _"Too glitchy to justify its surprisingly high cost, but it has some useful features. ... Good idea, poor execution. Try the free trial before you pay."_
- Liked: data-rich dashboard, multi-bank consolidation, welcome-bonus tracker, custom point values.
- Disliked: syncing failed on at least one card; Amex offers weren't activated; can't search merchants by brand (location only); failed to track credits already used; welcome-bonus tracker had wrong spend; wrong earn rate (1x vs correct 3x dining); omitted certain rewards (BofA 10% bonus, SimplyMiles).
- Notes the 7-day trial is effectively 4–5 days because full sync takes 48–72 hours. Cheapest plan $54/yr.

### Blog review — Miles to Memories (Ryan Smith), "The Skeleton Key…"
- Verdict: good concept; reviewer (an experienced points person) decided **not** to keep it because he already optimizes manually — but says it could suit people who want automation or a less-engaged spouse.
- Praised aggregating all bank offers, "ending soonest" view, location-based best-card.
- Criticized: needing the paid plan for the best features; **a week-long struggle to add Chase cards**; everything is in-app with no email alerts; confusing "set your own price" ($5/mo annual floor, up to $25/mo).
- Reader comments (a useful sentiment sample):
  - _"My issue is it keeps losing connectivity to Chase. And sometimes Amex. For weeks ... I'm not sure it's worth paying for."_ (George)
  - _"I cannot see getting $144 worth of value from this in a year ... interested if it was $10–20 a year."_ (John)
  - _"You sure lost me at 'provide them your bank login info.'"_ (Ella) / _"There is ZERO chance I'm providing an app with my bank login info."_ (GFB)
  - A developer in the space alleged the app logs in as you from its servers with stored credentials and isn't tokenized via OAuth — _"Scary stuff."_ (Emmanuel). MaxRewards replied it uses two-key encryption and doesn't store login data.
  - Notes _"Their reviews are littered with 1-star ratings due to connection problems"_ as Chase blocks the login method.

### Blog review — Kudos (competitor; May 2026), "Is It Worth $60/Year?"
> ⚠️ **Bias flag:** Kudos sells a competing product, so its conclusion (favoring Kudos) is self-interested. The factual testing notes are still useful.
- Ran MaxRewards + Kudos for 30 days with 7 cards. Found genuine value in **auto-activation** ($127 used of $340 available offers; ~16 hrs/yr saved) and **unified credit tracking**.
- Reported problems: cards failed to sync ~8 times in 30 days (Chase/Amex needing 2FA every few days); incorrect earn-rate data; location-only merchant search; the free tier (5 cards, no automation) is "essentially useless," making it effectively a $60/yr minimum.
- Bottom line per Kudos: worth it only for **power users with 6–10+ cards spending $400+/mo at offer merchants**; most users get more from free alternatives.

## Bottom line

MaxRewards is a **well-liked app with a real, sticky core value** — one dashboard for many cards, a "best card to use" engine, and automatic offer/credit activation that power users love (hence the ~4.5–4.7 star average). But the **most common, repeated complaints are operational**: cards (especially Chase, then Amex/Citi/Discover) disconnect constantly and sometimes trigger bank fraud locks; data is frequently inaccurate (wrong earn rates, misidentified cards, missed credits); and many feel the **Gold price ($54–$300/yr) is high for a data-consolidation tool**. The **credentials-based login model** is a recurring trust concern. Net: most reviewers say it's a great idea whose value depends heavily on (a) how many cards you carry and how much you spend at offer merchants, and (b) whether your specific banks stay synced. Standard advice across reviews: **use the free trial first**.
